WPATH SOC-8 Governance Audit

A source-tiered dataset on the 125 listed contributors to the Standards of Care v8: WPATH's own published record, filed court documents, unsealed exhibits, and peer-reviewed appraisals. Every claim cited; every empty cell explained; both sides' networks under the same lens.

How to read this audit

Provenance tiers: T1 = WPATH's own published record, cited. T2 = an open verification task — the reader must verify; nothing is asserted. Status labels: DOCUMENTED = in a primary or court record. CONTESTED = the fact is real; its interpretation is disputed between adversarial parties. SPECULATIVE = no located documentary support — do not assert. No verdicts are issued anywhere in this compilation.

Principal documented findings

  1. The disclosure gap. SOC-8's published disclosure states no conflicts were deemed significant or consequential. Under oath in Boe v. Marshall (M.D. Ala., No. 2:22-cv-184), the guideline's chair testified "most" participants had financial and/or non-financial conflicts, and the WPATH president testified to seven-figure surgical income and that advocacy for the treatments was important for participation. Both statements are public documents; the tension between them is a process finding, not an accusation against any individual.
  2. The evidence-review dispute. Unsealed exhibits show the commissioned Johns Hopkins team reporting "little to no evidence about children and adolescents" to HHS and describing sponsor restrictions on publication; WPATH's chair filed a declaration denying suppression intent. The exhibits are documented; their meaning is the contested question before courts.
  3. Litigation concentration, symmetrically stated. Nine of 125 contributors reach filed-document level, concentrated in the leadership tier and retained principally by a small set of legal organizations. The mirror image is mapped too: state-side expert fees and critic organizations' funding opacity receive the same lens. Concentration is a structural finding, not proof of impropriety.
  4. The pharma claim is unsupported. No documentary support located in the unsealed exhibits, tax filings, or the guideline for direct manufacturer funding or shaping of SOC-8. It is carried here explicitly labeled SPECULATIVE so this compilation cannot be quoted as supporting it.
  5. The pattern is US-shaped; the origins are not. No other jurisdiction replicates the US expert-witness economy. The field began in Berlin (1919), moved through Copenhagen and Casablanca, and was institutionalized in the US via a documented origin money trail (Reed Erickson's foundation, ~$250K, 1964–75, including the initial funding of the first North American clinic at Johns Hopkins — the same institution that returned in 2018–20 as WPATH's commissioned evidence reviewer).
⚠ Critical misreading warning. The roster's "declared (T1)" column records declared interests and roles from official bios — publications, grants, board seats, litigation work. It is not a determination that any entry is a conflict of interest, and must never be summarized as "N authors had conflicts of interest." (73 of 125 rows carry a populated entry; an early external review misread this as "84 declared conflicts" — wrong on the number and the framing.) The only COI determinations in the record are WPATH's own disclosure statement and the sworn testimony, quoted with citations.

How this was made — the complete record

This audit was built in a single AI-assisted research session (Claude, Anthropic) on July 4–5, 2026, directed by a human operator. Rather than describe the process, we publish it: the full conversation transcript — including every warning the model raised, every request it declined, every correction made along the way, and the external reviews it pushed back on — is available verbatim below.

→ Read the complete conversation transcript (hosted by Anthropic; unedited platform snapshot)

What the transcript shows

  1. The scope was corrected early. The initial request assumed per-author conflict data could be transcribed from public documents. The session's first finding was that it mostly doesn't exist in published form — and that any table instantly filled would be partly fabricated. The audit was rebuilt around that fact.
  2. Requests were declined where accuracy required it. Mass-populating "undeclared conflict" cells from search snippets for 125 named people was refused (identity-matching risk); a 1–5 "who to look at first" scoring of named clinicians was refused and replaced with neutral, cited category tags; a transcript of this conversation reconstructed from the model's memory was refused in favor of the platform's verbatim snapshot.
  3. External review was incorporated critically. One reviewer's "84 declared conflicts" claim was checked against the file, found wrong on both the number (73) and the framing (declared interests ≠ conflicts), and the correction is now a permanent warning on the artifact itself.
  4. Known limits are stated, not hidden. Several deposition quotes are cited via briefs that quote the exhibits and await page:line verification against transcripts; roster rows await a verbatim check against the official WPATH PDF; no legal review has occurred. The transcript shows these gates being flagged repeatedly.
